💧What is Vapour Pressure?

Every liquid has molecules that escape from its surface into the vapour phase. These escaping molecules exert a pressure on the walls of the container — this is called vapour pressure.

The vapour pressure of a pure solvent is the maximum pressure exerted by its vapour at a given temperature.

🧠 Simple Idea — Think of a Crowd!

Imagine a room full of people (solvent molecules). Some people keep trying to escape through the door (surface). The pushing and jostling at the door is the "vapour pressure."

Now mix some strangers (solute molecules) in the room. They block some exits — fewer people escape → less vapour pressure. That's exactly what happens!

🧪What Happens When We Add a Solute?
1
In a pure solvent, all surface positions are occupied by solvent molecules. Vapour pressure = P°1 (maximum).
2
When a non-volatile solute is dissolved, some surface is now occupied by solute molecules. Fewer solvent molecules can escape.
3
The vapour pressure of the solution (P1) is now less than P°1.
4
This decrease (P°1 − P1) is called Lowering of Vapour Pressure (ΔP).
📐Raoult's Law (Board Standard)

For a solution containing a non-volatile solute, Raoult's Law states:

Raoult's Law
P1 = x1 × P°1
Vapour pressure of solution = Mole fraction of solvent × VP of pure solvent

Where x1 = mole fraction of solvent = n1 / (n1 + n2)

⬇️Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure

The Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure is defined as the ratio of the lowering of vapour pressure to the vapour pressure of the pure solvent:

Definition
(P°1 − P1) / P°1 = x2
Relative lowering of VP = Mole fraction of solute
Derivation from Raoult's Law
P1 = x1·P°1
P°1 − P1 = P°1 − x1·P°1
ΔP = P°1(1 − x1)
ΔP/P°1 = 1 − x1 = x2
Since x₁ + x₂ = 1, therefore 1 − x₁ = x₂ (mole fraction of solute)

This is a Colligative Property — it depends only on the number of solute particles, not their identity.

🔑Key Points to Remember
✅ HSC BOARD IMPORTANT

Relative lowering of VP = Mole fraction of solute (x₂). This is a pure number — no units.

✅ HSC BOARD IMPORTANT

This applies only to non-volatile, non-electrolyte solutes in dilute solutions.

✅ HSC BOARD IMPORTANT

As solute amount increases → x₂ increases → Relative lowering increases → Solution VP decreases more.

✅ COLLIGATIVE PROPERTY

Relative lowering depends on the number of solute molecules, not their nature or size.

📈Vapour Pressure vs Mole Fraction of Solute (x₂)

As x₂ (mole fraction of solute) increases, the vapour pressure of the solution (P₁) decreases linearly. This is the graphical representation of Raoult's Law.

📌What the Graph Tells Us
1
At x₂ = 0 (pure solvent): P₁ = P°₁ — maximum vapour pressure
2
As x₂ increases: P₁ decreases — more solute → less VP
3
The graph is a straight line with negative slope = −P°₁

🔣Every Symbol Explained
SymbolFull NameMeaning in Simple Words
P°1 Vapour pressure of pure solvent Pressure exerted by the vapour of pure liquid solvent. This is the MAXIMUM value.
P1 Vapour pressure of solution Vapour pressure after dissolving solute. Always less than P°₁ for non-volatile solute.
ΔP Lowering of vapour pressure How much the VP has dropped: ΔP = P°₁ − P₁. Always positive.
ΔP/P°1 Relative lowering of VP Fraction of original VP that was lost. No units. Equal to x₂.
x1 Mole fraction of solvent Fraction of total moles that are solvent molecules. x₁ = n₁/(n₁+n₂)
x2 Mole fraction of solute Fraction of total moles that are solute molecules. x₂ = n₂/(n₁+n₂)
n1 Moles of solvent Number of moles of the liquid solvent in the solution.
n2 Moles of solute Number of moles of dissolved substance (solute).
🧠Memory Trick
🔑 How to Remember the Formula

"Relative Lowering = x₂"

Think: "Relative Lowering of VP is the RELATIVE amount of solute in the mix — which is exactly the mole fraction of solute (x₂)!"

Mole fraction of solute = How "relatively" much solute is there = Relative lowering of VP. 🎯

✏️ The One Equation That Gives You All Answers

(P°₁ − P₁) / P°₁ = x₂ = n₂ / (n₁ + n₂)

From this one equation, you can find P₁, P°₁, ΔP, x₂, n₁, or n₂ — whatever the question asks!
