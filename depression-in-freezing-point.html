Colligative Property — ΔTf — How solutes lower the freezing point

What is Freezing Point Depression?
"The decrease in the freezing point of a solvent when a non-volatile solute is dissolved in it"
The more solute you add → the lower the freezing point drops
ΔTf = Kf × m
For Non-Electrolytes (e.g. glucose, urea, sugar)

ΔTf = i × Kf × m
For Electrolytes (e.g. NaCl, CaCl2) — they split into ions
💡 Simple Idea — Why does freezing point drop?

Pure water freezes at 0°C. When you dissolve sugar or salt in it, the solute particles get in the way of water molecules trying to arrange themselves into ice crystals.

So the water needs to be cooled to a lower temperature before it can freeze. The more solute particles you add, the more they block ice formation — and the freezing point drops further.

This drop is called Depression in Freezing Point (ΔTf).

Real-Life Examples
🚗 Antifreeze in Car Radiators

Cars in cold countries add ethylene glycol (antifreeze) to the radiator water. This depresses the freezing point well below 0°C, so the coolant doesn't freeze even in -20°C winters. Same chemistry as ΔTf!

🛣️ Salt on Icy Roads

Road crews spread NaCl or CaCl2 on icy roads. The salt dissolves in the surface water, depresses its freezing point below 0°C, and melts the ice even when the air temperature is below 0°C. CaCl2 works better — can you guess why? (i = 3!)

🌊 Why Sea Water Freezes at −1.8°C

Ocean water contains dissolved salts (~3.5%). This depresses its freezing point from 0°C down to about −1.8°C. Polar ice forms because the temperature there drops even lower.

📝 Board Exam Formula Summary:
ΔTf = Kf × m → non-electrolytes (i = 1)
ΔTf = i × Kf × m → electrolytes
Tf (solution) = Tf (solvent) − ΔTf
where m = molality = moles of solute ÷ kg of solvent

ΔTf is a colligative property — depends only on the number of particles, NOT on their nature.

📊 What the graph tells us

The graph shows that the depression in freezing point (ΔTf) increases linearly with molality. The slope of each line equals i × Kf.

A steeper slope means more particles (higher i), which causes a greater depression. NaCl (i=2) gives a slope twice as steep as glucose (i=1)!

All Symbols Explained
SymbolFull NameMeaning & Unit
ΔTf Depression in Freezing Point How many degrees the freezing point drops. Unit: °C or K
Kf Cryoscopic Constant / Molal Freezing Point Depression Constant A constant that depends only on the solvent. Water = 1.86, Benzene = 5.12. Unit: K·kg·mol⁻¹
m Molality Moles of solute per kg of solvent. m = W₂/(M₂ × W₁) × 1000. Unit: mol/kg or mol·kg⁻¹
i van't Hoff Factor Number of particles one formula unit gives on dissolution. Glucose→ i=1, NaCl → i=2, CaCl₂ → i=3
Tf Freezing Point of Solution Tf (solution) = Tf (pure solvent) − ΔTf
T°f Freezing Point of Pure Solvent Water = 0°C, Benzene = 5.5°C, Acetic acid = 16.6°C
W1 Mass of Solvent In grams (g) or kilograms (kg)
W2 Mass of Solute In grams (g)
M2 Molar Mass of Solute In g/mol. Used to find molality from mass data
Complete Formula Chain
m = (W₂ × 1000) / (M₂ × W₁)
ΔTf = i × Kf × m
Tf(solution) = T°f − ΔTf
For finding molar mass: M₂ = (Kf × W₂ × 1000) / (ΔTf × W₁)
🧠 Memory Tricks

CRYOscopic = CRYO = ICE → Kf is about ICE / freezing
