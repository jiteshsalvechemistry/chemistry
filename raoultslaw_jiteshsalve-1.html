RAOULT'S LAW

Liquid–Liquid Solutions · Volatile Components

Interactive Simulation by Jitesh Salve
What is Raoult's Law?
Pₐ = P°ₐ · xₐ
Pb = P°b · xb
Partial VP of each component = VP of pure liquid × its mole fraction
Pt = Pₐ + Pb
Total Vapour Pressure = Sum of partial pressures (Dalton's Law)
The Simple Idea 💡
When you mix two volatile liquids A and B together, each liquid tries to evaporate (turn into vapour). The pressure each creates above the solution depends on:
  • How much of it was in the original pure form (P° — pure vapour pressure)
  • How much of it is present in the mixture (x — mole fraction)

The more of liquid A you add, the more vapour pressure A contributes. Simple!
Two Important Cases

Case 1: P°ₐ > P°b

Liquid A is more volatile than B.
Formula: Pt = (P°ₐ − P°b)·xₐ + P°b
Graph: Line rises from P°b (when xₐ=0) to P°ₐ (when xₐ=1)

Case 2: P°b > P°ₐ

Liquid B is more volatile than A.
Formula: Pt = (P°b − P°ₐ)·xb + P°ₐ
Graph: Line rises from P°ₐ (when xb=0) to P°b (when xb=1)
📌 Key Condition: Both liquids A and B must be volatile (i.e., they evaporate easily). If one component is non-volatile, we use a different form of Raoult's Law.

How to read this graph:
• The x-axis shows xₐ (mole fraction of A): 0 = pure B, 1 = pure A.
• The blue line is Pₐ = P°ₐ · xₐ (straight, starts at 0)
• The orange line is Pb = P°b · xb (straight, starts high)
• The green line is Pt = Pₐ + Pb (also straight for ideal solutions)
• The red dot is your current mixture from the Simulate tab!
What Does Each Symbol Mean?
P°ₐ
Vapour Pressure of Pure Liquid AWhen liquid A is alone (no mixing), this is the pressure its vapour creates at a given temperature.
P°b
Vapour Pressure of Pure Liquid BSame idea for liquid B alone.
xₐ
Mole Fraction of A in SolutionFraction = (moles of A) ÷ (total moles). Always between 0 and 1.
xb
Mole Fraction of B in Solutionxb = 1 − xₐ (they must always add up to 1)
Pₐ
Partial Vapour Pressure of AThe VP that A contributes to the mixture. Pₐ = P°ₐ × xₐ
Pb
Partial Vapour Pressure of BThe VP that B contributes to the mixture. Pb = P°b × xb
Pt
Total Vapour PressureSum of all partial pressures. Pt = Pₐ + Pb (by Dalton's Law)
🧠 Trick to Remember:
Think of a pizza 🍕 being shared. If A takes 60% of the pizza (xₐ = 0.6), and A's pure pressure was 100 mmHg — then A's contribution is 60% of 100 = 60 mmHg. That's exactly what Raoult's Law says!
