What is Elevation in Boiling Point?
"The increase in the boiling point of a solvent when a non-volatile solute is dissolved in it"
More solute you add → higher the boiling point rises
ΔTb = Kb × m
For Non-Electrolytes (e.g. glucose, urea, sugar)

ΔTb = i × Kb × m
For Electrolytes (e.g. NaCl, CaCl2)
💡 Simple Idea — Why Does Boiling Point Rise?

Pure water boils at 100°C. When you dissolve salt or sugar in it, the solute particles sit between water molecules and reduce the vapour pressure of the solution.

Since boiling happens when vapour pressure equals atmospheric pressure, the solution needs to be heated to a higher temperature to reach that point. The more solute you add, the more the boiling point rises.

This rise is called Elevation in Boiling Point (ΔTb).

Connection with Vapour Pressure
🔗 Why Boiling Point Rises — The Deep Reason

Boiling occurs when vapour pressure of liquid = atmospheric pressure (1 atm).

When solute is added → vapour pressure of solution decreases (Raoult's Law).
So the solution must be heated more to bring vapour pressure back to 1 atm.
This extra heating needed = ΔTb.

Real-Life Examples
🍝 Cooking Pasta — Adding Salt to Water

When you add salt to boiling water while cooking pasta, you slightly raise the boiling point. The water boils at a slightly higher temperature, cooking the pasta faster and more evenly. The effect is small (only about 0.5°C for normal cooking salt amounts) but real!

🚗 Antifreeze in Car Radiators

Ethylene glycol (antifreeze) added to car radiator water raises the boiling point from 100°C to about 130°C. This prevents the coolant from boiling away during engine operation. Same compound also lowers the freezing point — dual benefit!

🧪 Why Kb is Used to Find Molar Mass

By measuring ΔTb of a solution with known mass of unknown solute, we can calculate the molar mass (M₂) of the solute:

M₂ = (Kb × W₂ × 1000) / (ΔTb × W₁)

This is a very important application asked in board exams!

📝 Board Exam Memory Trick:
ΔTb = Kb × m → non-electrolytes (i = 1)
ΔTb = i × Kb × m → electrolytes
Tb (solution) = Tb (solvent) + ΔTb

Memory: "Kb Keep Boiling" — Kb of water = 0.52
Compare with Kf = 1.86 — Kb is always smaller than Kf for water

ΔTb is a colligative property — depends only on the NUMBER of particles, NOT their nature.

📊 What the Graph Tells Us

ΔTb increases linearly with molality. The slope = i × Kb. Higher i means steeper slope — more particles, greater elevation. The graph always passes through the origin (pure solvent, no elevation).

📝 For Board Exam Graph:

Y-axis = Elevation in Boiling Point (ΔTb) in °C or K
Straight line through origin. Slope = Kb (for i=1). Greater i → steeper line.

🧠 Memory Tricks

EBULLIOscopic = EBULLIO = BOIL (Latin: ebullire = to boil) → Kb is about BOILING

Kb of water = 0.52 → Always smaller than Kf (1.86). Think: boiling needs less push than freezing.

"i Keeps Boiling More" → i × Kb × m

Compare the two colligative properties:
ΔTf = i × Kf × m → FREEZING POINT goes DOWN (−)
ΔTb = i × Kb × m → BOILING POINT goes UP (+)

Kf (1.86) is always greater than Kb (0.52) for water — freezing point depresses more than boiling point elevates for same solution.
