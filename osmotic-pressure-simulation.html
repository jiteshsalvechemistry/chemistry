What is Osmotic Pressure?
"The minimum external pressure that must be applied on a solution to prevent the entry of solvent into it through a semipermeable membrane"
In simple words: the pressure needed to STOP osmosis
π = CRT
π = Osmotic Pressure (atm or Pa)
C = Molar Concentration (mol L−1)
R = 0.0821 L·atm·mol−1·K−1
T = Temperature in Kelvin

For Electrolytes: π = iCRT    (i = van't Hoff factor)
💡 Simple Idea — What is Osmosis?

Imagine a semipermeable membrane like a net — it lets only water molecules pass through, not the dissolved particles (solute).

Water always travels from a region of low solute concentration (lots of water) → to high solute concentration (less water).

The pressure that builds up due to this water movement is called osmotic pressure (π).

Three Types of Tonicity
⚖️ ISOTONIC
Isotonic Solution
Concentration outside = Concentration inside cell
• No net movement of water
• RBC keeps its normal biconcave shape
• Water enters and exits at equal rates
• Example: 0.9% NaCl (Normal Saline) — used in IV drips
• Osmotic pressure of solution = osmotic pressure of cell
📉 HYPERTONIC
Hypertonic Solution
Concentration outside > Concentration inside cell
• Water moves OUT of the cell (exosmosis)
• RBC shrinks and becomes spiky — called crenation
• Cell loses water to the surrounding solution
• Example: Seawater, 10% NaCl solution, Concentrated sugar
• Osmotic pressure outside > osmotic pressure of cell
📈 HYPOTONIC
Hypotonic Solution
Concentration outside < Concentration inside cell
• Water moves INTO the cell (endosmosis)
• RBC swells and may burst — called haemolysis
• Cell gains water from the surrounding solution
• Example: Distilled water, pure water
• Osmotic pressure outside < osmotic pressure of cell
🩸 Why Doctors Use 0.9% Saline for IV Drips

Blood plasma is isotonic with 0.9% NaCl. If we use pure water (hypotonic), the RBCs would swell and burst. If we use too much salt (hypertonic), RBCs would shrink. So doctors always use isotonic saline — it matches the blood exactly and keeps cells safe!

📝 Board Exam Memory Trick:
ISO = same (isobar, isotope — "same" in Greek)
HYPER = more/above (hypertension = high BP)
HYPO = less/below (hypothermia = low temp)

Water always runs from HYPO → HYPER (from less to more solute)

🌊 Real-Life Analogy:
Think of osmotic pressure like water flowing downhill. The "hill" is solute concentration — water always flows from the low side (dilute) to the high side (concentrated). The more concentrated the outside solution, the more the cell loses water — and the more it shrinks!
Symbols & Definitions
Symbol Full Name Simple Meaning
π Osmotic Pressure Pressure needed to stop osmosis (in atm or Pa)
C Molar Concentration How many moles of solute per litre of solution (mol/L)
R Gas Constant 0.0821 L·atm·mol−1·K−1 — a universal fixed number
T Temperature Temperature in Kelvin (T = °C + 273)
i van't Hoff Factor Number of particles one formula unit gives when dissolved
(NaCl → Na+ + Cl−, so i = 2)
SPM Semipermeable Membrane A membrane that allows ONLY solvent (water) to pass, not solute
πout Osmotic Pressure (outside) Osmotic pressure of the external solution
πin Osmotic Pressure (inside) Osmotic pressure of the cell fluid (≈ 0.9% NaCl for RBC)
Van't Hoff Equation
π = iCRT

NaCl → i = 2  |  CaCl2 → i = 3
Glucose (non-electrolyte) → i = 1
🧠 Memory Trick for Tonicity

ISOtonic = ISObar/ISOperimeter → SAME
HYPERtonic = HYPERactive → MORE than normal
HYPOtonic = HYPOthermic → LESS than normal

"Water follows the crowd" — water always moves toward where there are MORE solute particles (higher concentration), just like people follow a crowd!

📋 Colligative Property:
Osmotic pressure is a colligative property — it depends only on the number of solute particles, not on the nature (identity) of the solute. More particles = higher osmotic pressure.
